Machu Picchu

Machu Picchu, the "Lost City of the Incas," is a masterpiece of ancient engineering and a testament to the ingenuity of the Inca civilization. Perched high in the Andes Mountains at 2,430 meters above sea level, the site was built in the 15th century during the reign of Emperor Pachacuti.

The purpose of Machu Picchu remains a mystery. Scholars believe it was a royal estate, ceremonial site, or retreat for the Inca elite. Its location, surrounded by towering peaks and lush greenery, adds to its mystique. The site features terraced fields, temples, and residential areas, all built using advanced stone-cutting techniques.

Notable structures include the Temple of the Sun, which aligns with the solstices, and the Intihuatana stone, thought to have been used for astronomical observations. Machu Picchu’s layout reflects the Incas’ deep understanding of astronomy and environmental harmony.

Rediscovered in 1911 by American explorer Hiram Bingham, Machu Picchu is now one of the most visited sites in South America. Its breathtaking views and intricate design attract travelers from around the globe, offering a glimpse into the grandeur of the Inca Empire.
