Chichen Itza (Mexico)

Chichen Itza, located on Mexico’s Yucatán Peninsula, was a major city of the Mayan civilization. Flourishing between the 9th and 12th centuries, it was a center for science, religion, and trade, blending Mayan and Toltec influences in its architecture.

The city’s centerpiece, El Castillo (the Temple of Kukulcan), is a step pyramid that serves as a remarkable example of Mayan astronomical knowledge. During the equinoxes, sunlight casts a shadow that resembles a serpent slithering along the pyramid’s steps, a tribute to the feathered serpent god Kukulcan.

Other significant structures include the Great Ball Court, where ceremonial ball games were played, and the Temple of the Warriors, adorned with carvings of warriors and jaguars. The Cenote Sagrado, a natural sinkhole, was used for rituals and sacrifices.

Chichen Itza reflects the complexity and sophistication of the Mayan civilization. Its enduring architecture and cultural significance make it one of the most visited archaeological sites in the Americas.
